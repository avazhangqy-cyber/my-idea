# Open Questions

## Main research question

How can a moderated anonymous web space combining scenario-based guidance, brief self-assessment, and peer stories support Chinese high school students in navigating self-identity-related concerns?

## Product questions

- Which concern do users choose most often: uncertainty, privacy fear, friend disclosure, family disclosure, bullying, or wanting peer stories?
- Which support type is most helpful: scenario guidance, brief self-assessment, resource navigation, or peer story sharing?
- Do users feel more understood after receiving a non-labeling response?
- Does the page feel safe enough for a high school student to use privately?
- What wording feels supportive without sounding like it is diagnosing the user?

## Data questions

- Which scenario pathway is selected most often?
- How do brief distress scores, perceived support scores, and safety-expression scores relate to the next step users say they would choose?
- Which story tags make users feel less isolated?
- Are there signs that the tool is too vague, too serious, too scary, too clinical, or not actionable enough?

## Ethics and safety questions

- How can the site avoid collecting sensitive personal identity data while still collecting useful product feedback?
- What crisis/support language should appear without making the page feel alarming?
- Should the site include links to crisis resources, school counseling, LGBTQ+ youth resources, or all three?
- How can the site make clear that it does not decide or test a user's sexual orientation?
- How can anonymous story sharing create peer support without becoming an unmoderated forum?
- What information must be removed before an anonymous story is published?

## Next research leads

- Look for studies on anonymous digital mental health support for adolescents.
- Look for research on LGBTQ+ youth help-seeking behavior and barriers to formal counseling.
- Compare existing youth resource pages to see which language feels most accessible to high school students.
- Ask 3-5 trusted peers to review only the wording, not share personal experiences.
- If using formal measures in the final paper, use official versions of PHQ-4 / GAD-2, WHO-5, MSPSS, and disclosure-related research rather than treating the prototype checklist as a validated diagnostic scale.
